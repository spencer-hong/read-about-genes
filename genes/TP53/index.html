The gene is involved in various biological processes, including negative regulation of transcription by RNA polymerase II, mitophagy, and chromatin organization. It enables functions such as transcription cis-regulatory region binding, RNA polymerase II cis-regulatory region sequence-specific DNA binding, and DNA-binding transcription factor activity, RNA polymerase II-specific. The gene is located in the nucleus, nucleoplasm, and cytoplasm, and is part of the transcription regulator complex and protein-containing complex. Additionally, it plays roles in processes like in utero embryonic development, somitogenesis, and release of cytochrome c from mitochondria, among others. The gene is also involved in the DNA damage response and acts upstream of or within the ER overload response. Furthermore, it enables binding activities such as protease binding, p53 binding, and DNA binding. The gene is involved in the regulation of various processes, including transcription by RNA polymerase II, apoptotic process, and cell cycle, among others.

TP53 is a multifunctional gene with roles as an activator, DNA-binding protein, and repressor. It is implicated in various diseases, including cancer-related genes, disease variants, Li-Fraumeni syndrome, and functions as a tumor suppressor. Evidence for its function is available at the protein level. TP53 is detected in all tissues and is primarily localized in the nucleoplasm, vesicles, and cytosol. Its expression is associated with Cluster 13 in blood, Cluster 36 in lymphoid tissue, Cluster 44 in the brain, Cluster 42 in cell lines, and Cluster 16 in single cells, specifically intestinal goblet cells involved in mucin production.

The top transcription factors (TFs) identified from the GTRD database of TF binding sites, based on the number of CHIP-seq experiments, include SMC3 with 6 experiments, FLI1 with 5 experiments, BRD4 with 4 experiments, GATA4 with 4 experiments, and GATA2 with 4 experiments. These TFs have been shown to play significant roles in gene regulation, as evidenced by their frequent appearance in CHIP-seq data.
